Serious games for people with mental disorders:
State of the art of practices to maintain engagement and accessibility

Presented by: Carlos Carrillo

Introduction

In today's world our increased awarness of mental disabilities and regularly improvments in technology has made it our responsibility to make sure we create products that are accessible for and targeted to those that are a part of these demographics. This study focused on analyzing serious games that were made specifically to help people with mental disabilities. They searched through various scientific journals to find games that met this criteria. From there they broke down their testing intofeatures proposed for accessible gaming by Game Accessibility Guidelines and Accessible Games. They were limited to observing games that were either publicly available or had enough screenshots or video playthrough in their articles. This reduced the pool to 17 games.

Conclusion

The results showed that even though serious games could be extremely useful in helping people with cognitive abilities develop skills for everyday life in a fun way, there are not many games targeted towards this population. It can be assumed that this is due to the difficulty in making games for said population since their disabilities can impact their understanding of the game and its mechanics. And of those games most seem to be targeted towards people with autism or dementia. It's also found that the basic recommendations from the accessibility guides are usually applied, like low number of input controls and the use of symbols, but the more complex recommendations usually aren't, like the use of multiplayer to help with socializing and practicing skills with others. Ultimately while there are many developments in the world of game accessibility and more video games are working with experts to take accessibility into consideration, there is still a lot of work to do.
